Passing Diminished Chords

04/09/2026

When researching contemporary Japanese music, I often see a misconception within the West that describes music made in Japan as non-Western harmony.

While this is true for classical Japanese music and Far East classical music in general, contemporary Japanese music follows the exact same harmonic standard as Western music.

So, why does it sound different? How can a Westerner listen to a J-Pop song and feel completely different than when listening to standard Western pop, and vice versa for a Japanese listener to Western music?

Basics

The most basic understanding I can employ is that Japanese music is actually no different than any Western music, as I noted with its harmonic structure. It's just an extremely niche and specific genre. You can think of this in the way that jazz music is different from rock music, but jazz music is not built off of a different harmonic standard - it's still modern Western harmony.

Knowing this, contemporary Japanese music is just like any typical musical genre - the songs within the genre use patterns, conventions, and themes that turn it into something standalone, but not a different harmonic practice.

What Makes it a Genre

There's a pretty extensive history that predates contemporary Japanese music and eventually leads up into the evolution and conception of what is now contemporary Japanese music.

It starts with classical Japanese music (hōgaku) which has been the country's musical culture for centuries. It wasn't until the Meiji era of Japan that Western influences started to shape the harmonic structure of the music and turn it into a more universal format.

As communications became more widespread across the globe, Japanese composers were introduced to early Western jazz and pop music. Over the course of the 20th century, this increase in popularity of Western music started to appear in Japanese compositions with the rise of 'city pop', where the earliest use of the Royal Road chord progression was used by Yumi Arai in the 1974 hit 'Yasashisa Ni Tsutsumareta Nara'.

It's this chord progression that settled a standout genre rooted in Western harmony for the Japanese people. The Royal Road progression, and then the Komuro progression, combined with the use of traditional Japanese musical conventions (pentatonic scales, specific Japanese mora-time lyrics) and the Western jazz influence, created the themes and conventions now heard in virtually all contemporary Japanese music.

Conclusion

In the end, you can really thank Western influence for creating the contemporary Japanese music genre, ironically. Without that transition into modern Western harmony and the heavy influence of jazz techniques, the current landscape of contemporary Japanese music may have looked completely different.

As was with my last post, this is just the tip of the iceberg when it comes to this niche. Japanese history as a whole spans the course of many centuries, so there's a lot more to learn about their musical culture, which I implore you to do.

To me, it's very important that this musical genre gets more documentation in the West and basically anywhere outside of the Far East, as it is heavily under-reported on and there's never been much research done on it for those studying music outside of its origin, and, even in the East, it's still not taught as any specific niche - because it is simply the norm.

If you'd like to get started learning more, I recommend reading 'The Royal Road Progression in Japanese Popular Music' by Maxwell Ramage, published by the Oxford University Press. It's a comprehensive study that delves into the intricacies of the genre (more specifically the Royal Road chord progression) and its historical context.
