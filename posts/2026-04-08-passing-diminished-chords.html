Passing Diminished Chords

04/08/2026

Welcome to my first blog post! This one's about one of my favorite chord techniques: passing diminished chords.

It might be somewhat of a boring first post, but it's been on my mind, and I'd like anybody else who stumbles upon this to know about passing diminished chords.

What Are Passing Diminished Chords?

A passing diminished chord is, as the name suggests, a diminished chord that is used to pass, slide, or connect to another note. It is typically used in music based in the roots of jazz, or other music with more complexity than simple diatonic harmony as seen in pop music.

How to Use Passing Diminished Chords

The basic idea is really quite simple.

You can take any chord in a chord progression, like C major, for example, and, in between that C major chord and the next chord in the progression, you can insert a diminished chord a half-step up or down (depending on target chord). If we were going up to D minor, this would be a C# diminished chord.

Typically, it's best to use passing diminished chords in upward motion, as this replicates the effect that the subtonic has moving to the tonic (B to C), but, as with all music theory principles, this is not a strict rule, and there are many cases where downward motion can be incredibly effective as well.

This post just touches the surface of passing diminished chords. That's not to say it's a greatly extensive subject, but there's a lot more you could learn about them, so after you're done here, I suggest you explore further and experiment with passing diminished chords in your own work.
